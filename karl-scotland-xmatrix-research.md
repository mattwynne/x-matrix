# Karl Scotland / X-Matrix research notes

## Executive summary

Karl Scotland’s X-Matrix work is a Lean/Agile strategy deployment approach built around a one-page A3 visual model. His current language for the model is **TASTE**:

- **True North** — the orienting direction or vision; decisions should move toward it.
- **Aspirations** — ambitious desired results; challenges rather than fixed targets.
- **Strategies** — guiding policies / enabling constraints that shape choices.
- **Tactics** — coherent actions or experiments to test hypotheses and implement strategies.
- **Evidence** — leading indicators/outcomes that show whether progress is happening.

The “X” comes from the four correlation matrices in the corners of the template, used to show relationships between the elements. Scotland emphasizes that the template alone is not enough: it needs collaborative strategy deployment, catchball, learning, and cadence.

## Key concepts

### Strategy deployment model

Scotland’s underlying model links four kinds of things:

1. **Results / Aspirations**: lagging indicators of organizational impact.
2. **Strategies**: enabling constraints that guide choices and focus attention.
3. **Outcomes / Evidence**: leading indicators that show whether strategies are working.
4. **Tactics**: experiments or work that should generate evidence and outcomes.

A useful framing: tactics do not directly “cause” aspirations; tactics are hypotheses that should generate evidence, which indicates whether strategies are helping achieve aspirations.

### TASTE

The TASTE acronym appears to be Scotland’s preferred modern framing for populating the X-Matrix:

- True North
- Aspirations
- Strategies
- Tactics
- Evidence

### Order of working through the matrix

Although TASTE is the mnemonic, Scotland’s practical facilitation order differs:

1. True North
2. Aspirations
3. Strategies
4. Correlations: Strategies → Aspirations
5. Evidence
6. Correlations: Evidence → Aspirations
7. Tactics
8. Correlations: Tactics → Strategies
9. Correlations: Tactics → Evidence

The notable move is to identify **Evidence before Tactics**. Scotland argues this helps avoid confirmation bias and stops teams from jumping to obvious pre-existing work before agreeing how they will know whether the strategy is working.

### Measurement stance

Scotland is careful to distinguish aspirations/evidence from hard targets:

- **Aspirations** express ambition and challenge, not prediction.
- **Evidence** is leading-indicator feedback — things to see more or less of — not a target imposed too early.
- This is meant to avoid “tyranny of the explicit” and strategy-by-platitude.

### Workshop/facilitation stance

The workshop pattern is contextual, but generally uses divergent and convergent thinking:

- explore the current situation;
- identify ambitions and challenges/opportunities;
- create strategies;
- define evidence;
- choose tactics/experiments;
- use correlations to test coherence and alignment.

Scotland describes the purpose as creating organizational alignment through collaborative strategy creation, and enabling autonomy through clear communication/deployment.

### Curiosity framework

Scotland’s later writing suggests you do not have to start with a full workshop or a filled A3. Start by using the X-Matrix as a **curiosity framework**: ask better questions about direction, ambition, choices, actions, and feedback.

### OKRs relationship

Scotland’s 2025 OKR/X-Matrix point: the important distinction is between **strategic objectives** and **SMART objectives**.

- Strategic objectives enable choice and exploration.
- SMART objectives are more explicit and specific.
- If OKRs are strategic, the X-Matrix can help identify the concrete tactics/experiments and evidence.
- If OKRs are already specific/SMART, be careful not to collapse strategy into a task list.
